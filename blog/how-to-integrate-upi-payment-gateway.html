How to Integrate UPI Payment Gateway — A Complete Guide

March 15, 2025 • By Dada Payment Team

What Is UPI?

The Unified Payments Interface (UPI) is a real-time payment system developed by the National Payments Corporation of India (NPCI). It allows users to link multiple bank accounts to a single mobile application and make instant fund transfers 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. UPI has transformed the way Indians pay for goods and services, processing over 10 billion transactions per month as of 2025.

UPI works by assigning each user a Virtual Payment Address (VPA) — for example, username@bankname — which eliminates the need to share sensitive bank details like account numbers and IFSC codes. This simplicity has driven mass adoption across urban and rural India alike.

Why UPI for Your Business

If you are targeting the Indian market, integrating a UPI payment gateway is not optional — it is essential. Here is why UPI should be at the top of your payment stack:

  • Massive user base: Over 350 million active UPI users across India, spanning every demographic.
  • Instant settlement: Funds move in real time, improving your cash flow and reducing reconciliation headaches.
  • Zero or minimal transaction fees: UPI transactions carry very low merchant fees compared to card networks, making it cost-effective at any scale.
  • Higher success rates: UPI transactions boast success rates above 99% on well-integrated platforms, far exceeding card payment success rates in India.
  • Mobile-first design: With over 700 million smartphone users in India, UPI fits naturally into mobile-first product experiences.
  • Regulatory push: The Reserve Bank of India actively promotes UPI, ensuring long-term stability and support for the platform.

Prerequisites for UPI Integration

Before you begin integrating UPI payments into your platform, ensure you have the following in place:

  1. Business registration: A legally registered business entity in India (Private Limited, LLP, or equivalent) with a valid GSTIN.
  2. Merchant bank account: A current (business) bank account with a UPI-participating bank.
  3. Payment gateway partner: A payment service provider (PSP) or payment aggregator that offers UPI integration APIs. Dada Payment provides a fully managed UPI integration through our Payment Gateway System.
  4. SSL certificate: Your application must serve all payment pages over HTTPS.
  5. Technical resources: A development team familiar with RESTful APIs, webhook handling, and secure credential management.
  6. KYC documentation: PAN card, address proof, and director identification documents for compliance verification.

Step-by-Step UPI Integration Guide

Step 1: Choose Your Integration Method

UPI can be integrated through several approaches depending on your platform architecture:

  • Collect request (Pull): Your server sends a payment request to the customer's UPI app. The customer approves it from their phone. This is the most common method for e-commerce and subscription billing.
  • Intent flow: Your app launches the customer's UPI application directly with pre-filled payment details. Ideal for mobile apps where you want a seamless one-tap experience.
  • QR code: Generate a dynamic QR code that customers scan with any UPI app to complete payment. Excellent for in-store, kiosk, or desktop checkout flows.

For most online businesses, the collect request or intent flow is recommended. QR codes work best as a complementary option for desktop users.

Step 2: Obtain API Credentials

After selecting your payment gateway partner, you will receive sandbox (test) credentials. These typically include:

  • Merchant ID
  • API Key and Secret
  • Sandbox endpoint URLs
  • Webhook signing key

Store these credentials securely using environment variables or a secrets manager — never hard-code them in your source code or commit them to version control.

Step 3: Implement the Payment Initiation Flow

Your backend server creates a payment order by calling the gateway's order creation API. A typical request includes the transaction amount, currency (INR), customer VPA, order reference ID, and a callback URL. The gateway validates the request, generates a transaction ID, and returns it along with the payment link or intent URI.

On the frontend, present the customer with their preferred UPI flow: an input field for their VPA (collect request), a button that triggers the UPI intent (mobile), or a QR code they can scan.

Step 4: Handle Webhooks and Callbacks

UPI payments are asynchronous. After the customer approves or rejects the payment in their UPI app, your gateway will notify your server via a webhook POST request. You must:

  • Verify the webhook signature using the signing key provided during onboarding.
  • Validate that the transaction amount and order reference match your records.
  • Update your order status (success, failed, or pending) accordingly.
  • Return a 200 OK response to acknowledge receipt of the webhook.

Implement idempotency checks to handle duplicate webhook deliveries gracefully.

Step 5: Handle Edge Cases and Failures

In production, things do not always go smoothly. Plan for:

  • Timeouts: UPI collect requests expire after a configurable window (usually 5–15 minutes). Show a countdown to the customer and allow retry.
  • Bank server errors: If the customer's bank is temporarily unreachable, guide them to try again or use an alternative payment method.
  • Duplicate payments: Use unique order reference IDs and server-side checks to prevent charging a customer twice.
  • Refunds: Implement automated and manual refund workflows via the gateway's refund API.

Step 6: Go Live

After thorough testing in the sandbox environment, request production credentials from your payment gateway partner. Before going live, verify that:

  • All API endpoints are updated to production URLs.
  • Webhook endpoints are publicly accessible and secured with HTTPS.
  • Error handling and retry logic have been validated.
  • Transaction logging and monitoring are in place.

Testing UPI Payments

Most payment gateway providers offer a sandbox environment that simulates UPI transactions without real money. During testing, verify the following scenarios:

  • Successful payment completion end to end.
  • Payment failure due to insufficient funds.
  • Payment timeout when the customer does not respond to the collect request.
  • Webhook delivery and signature verification.
  • Refund processing for partial and full amounts.
  • Concurrent transaction handling under load.

Common Integration Challenges

Teams integrating UPI for the first time often encounter these challenges:

  • VPA validation: Not all VPAs are valid or active. Implement VPA verification before initiating a collect request to reduce failed transactions.
  • Callback URL issues: Webhooks may fail if your server is behind a firewall, has strict CORS policies, or does not respond within the gateway's timeout window.
  • Transaction status ambiguity: In rare cases, a transaction may remain in “pending” state. Implement a polling mechanism to check transaction status periodically via the gateway's status API.
  • Regulatory compliance: Ensure your UPI integration complies with RBI guidelines on data localization, two-factor authentication, and transaction limits.
  • Multi-bank routing: Distributing transactions across multiple acquiring banks improves success rates but adds complexity to your integration layer.

Best Practices

  1. Offer multiple UPI flows: Support both collect request and QR code to accommodate different user preferences and device types.
  2. Show real-time status: Display a live transaction status screen so customers know their payment is being processed.
  3. Implement smart retries: If a collect request times out, allow the customer to retry without re-entering their VPA.
  4. Monitor success rates: Track UPI success rates by bank, time of day, and transaction amount. Alert your team when rates drop below thresholds.
  5. Keep SDKs updated: Payment gateway SDKs are updated regularly to address security patches and new UPI features. Stay current.
  6. Plan for scale: UPI transaction volumes can spike dramatically during sales events. Ensure your infrastructure and gateway plan can handle peak loads.
